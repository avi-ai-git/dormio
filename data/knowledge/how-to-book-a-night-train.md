# How to book a night train in Europe

Night trains are sold by the operator that runs them, not by a single Europe-wide shop. Find the operator for your route first, then book on that operator's own site, where the fares and the sleeper inventory are most complete.

The booking window matters. Most operators open sales two to six months before departure. ÖBB Nightjet opens about six months ahead. Sleepers and the cheapest fares sell out first, so popular summer routes and festive-season trains are worth booking the day they open.

Accommodation is tiered and priced separately. A seat is cheapest, a couchette (a shared berth in a 4 or 6 person compartment) is mid-range, and a private sleeper with a bed, and sometimes a shower and toilet, is the most expensive. You book a specific class, not just a ticket.

Cross-border trains can be cheaper when bought from a different operator. A Germany to Austria Nightjet is often cheaper on DB's Sparpreis Europa fare than on ÖBB, for the same train. It is worth checking both ends of an international route.

Hold a printed or downloaded ticket and your passport for routes that leave the Schengen area. Some operators still check tickets at the platform rather than the barrier.
